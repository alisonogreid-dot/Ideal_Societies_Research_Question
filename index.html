1What makes a research question researchable?

~10 min B.i scaffolding

Your summative research paper in a few weeks will open with your own research question. That question will shape everything that follows — the sources you find, the evidence you use, the argument you build.

Today's job is to practise writing one. Low stakes. Nothing you write today is locked in.

The weak vs. strong test

A research question isn't just "a question about your topic." Some questions are researchable — they push you to gather evidence and form an argument. Others dead-end quickly.

✗ Dead-end question

"What is the HDI?"

You can answer this in one sentence from a definition. There's nothing to argue about.

✓ Researchable question

"To what extent does a high HDI guarantee a good quality of life for citizens?"

You need evidence from multiple countries. Different sources will disagree. You'll have to take a position.

✗ Dead-end question

"Is Norway a good country?"

"Good" is too vague. Good for whom? Measured how? You'll flail.

✓ Researchable question

"How does judicial independence in democracies like Norway protect the fairness citizens experience?"

Specific factor (judicial independence). Specific outcome (fairness). Forces evidence.

The anatomy of a strong research question

Most strong research questions in social sciences have three or four parts:

Question stem
+
Priority/angle
+
Factor(s)
+
Scope

Example: "To what extent [stem] does protecting individual freedoms [priority] depend on citizen participation and a free press [factors] in modern European democracies [scope]?"

Strong research questions usually start with one of these stems. Each one pushes you to do something specific:

  • To what extent… → forces you to weigh evidence for and against a claim (command term: evaluate)
  • How does X affect Y… → forces you to explain a relationship (command term: explain)
  • Why do some societies… → forces comparison and causal reasoning
  • How effective is X in achieving Y… → forces judgement against criteria (command term: evaluate)
  • What role does X play in… → forces you to connect one factor to a bigger system

Avoid "Is X good/bad?" and "Yes/no" questions — they collapse too quickly.

C · Write the full question

Now combine: question stem + priority + factor + scope. Aim for one clear sentence.

0 words
  • "To what extent does [factor] determine whether citizens experience [outcome] in [scope]?"
  • "How does [factor] protect [priority] in [scope]?"
  • "Why do some countries achieve high [outcome] despite low [factor]?"
  • "What role does [factor] play in creating fair societies, compared to [another factor]?"
  • "How effective is [factor] in ensuring [priority] for [specific group/region]?"

3Peer feedback round

~12 min Structured protocol

You'll swap devices with a partner and type your feedback directly into their screen. Read the protocol first, then swap.

The feedback protocol

1 · Read silently

Read your partner's question twice. Don't talk yet. Don't type yet.

2 · Check the anatomy

Can you spot the four parts?

  • Question stem (To what extent / How does / Why do…)
  • Priority (which lens?)
  • Factor (which one from the unit?)
  • Scope (which country/region/timeframe?)

If a part is missing, name which one.

3 · Stress-test it

Ask yourself:

  • Could you answer this in one sentence from a definition? If yes → too narrow.
  • Is it too big to answer in 2–3 pages? If yes → too broad.
  • Would different sources disagree on the answer? If no → not much to argue.
  • Does a command term (evaluate, explain, describe) fit naturally? If no → the question may be fuzzy.
4 · Type feedback in this exact form

Write exactly two things:

  1. One strength: "Your question works because ___."
  2. One specific suggestion: "I'd sharpen ___ by ___."

No "great question!" No "looks good." Be specific.
